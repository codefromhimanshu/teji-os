# Company Constitution — Our Foundation

> The thinking we operate from, and the why beneath it. We return to this often
> to stay sharp, aligned, and on course.

## Why this exists

What broke us the first time was not skill — it was that we started with no
foundation. The whole idea was copy something, sell it, make money, and that
hollow center made everything downstream go loose: we ran on motivation,
motivation needed money that never came, and then everything slackened. The
endless discussions, the drift, the wrong bets — those were symptoms. The
disease was that we never decided what we actually stood on. This document
fixes the disease.

This is one way of operating, not a claim that it is the only one that works.
Not every startup can be a democracy, especially this early, and this one is
not. Most of what holds us together below is not faith in either founder — it
is a system: frozen scope, kill rules, success defined before we launch, the
work kept visible. The system does the heavy lifting.

## What we are doing

We are here to build something at civilisation scale. We do not yet know its
shape, and that is on purpose: we find it by building toward it, not by
defining it first.

- **The main idea**: put ourselves out in the world by building things and
  showing them. The opportunities we are after — people, feedback, ideas,
  partners — come from doing the work in the open, not from a room. x.com is
  where we show what we are doing.
- **Discipline, not motivation.** We show up because we decided to, not because
  it feels good that week. Motivation and momentum run out. The decision to
  keep going does not.
- **Our momentum is the velocity of our experimentation**: how fast we launch,
  learn, fail, and succeed. We work to raise it over time.

## How we work together

### Who does what

Direction is the founder's: what we build, what we prioritize, what good means,
the standards we hold. Once a standard is set, it is set — it gets built
within, not re-decided each time.

### How decisions get made

The founder makes the final call, and because of that, the failures are his to
own and learn from. On any big decision, the partner puts his thinking down in
writing: a clear, thought-out case. It gets read properly, questioned as
needed; the case may challenge the decision hard. Then the discussion is
closed and the founder decides — sometimes the partner's way, sometimes not,
sometimes taking a piece. Either way we stop there and we build.

We do not circle the same decision for weeks, relitigating and convincing. Say
your piece well, once, in writing. Then we move.

### Trust through the mistakes

The founder will get decisions wrong, some badly. That is the plan — make
mistakes fast, own them, learn. Do not talk him out of them after the call is
made.

**The one exception — the irreversibility brake.** This applies only to
mistakes we can recover from, which is almost all of them. Some decisions are
hard or impossible to take back: a long contract or lock-in, giving away
equity or control, legal exposure, publishing something that damages our name,
losing data we cannot recover, burning a relationship or channel we will need
later. On those, the partner does not just get to hit the brakes hard — he is
expected to. Reality gets a vote on what we cannot take back.

### What the founder can be held to

1. He decides quickly, so no one is blocked waiting on him.
2. He gives the reasoning, not just the call.
3. He changes his mind when the evidence changes.
4. He never asks anyone to keep faith in something the experiment has already
   buried.

Holding him to these is the partner's most important job. On whether the
founder is living up to what is written here, the authority is the partner's,
not the founder's.

**The heaviest rule — judgment on the table.** If we keep killing cleanly,
experiment after experiment, at some point the thing to examine is no longer
the experiments but the founder's judgment — the assumptions generating these
bets. When failures pile up despite the process holding, his judgment goes on
the table next, and the partner is expected to put it there.

### Authority grows with territory

Authority is earned into existence as the thing it would govern comes to
exist: the day there is an engineering team, running it is the partner's; as a
domain becomes real and proven, the calls inside it become his to make, not
just to shape.

## The engine we run

One loop, repeating: pick a bet, freeze it, run it inside a fixed window, call
an honest verdict, feed the next bet. Most bets stay small. One may grow into
the big thing. The speed of that loop is the whole game.

### How the big thing comes

Two routes: the long upfront bet (needs resources and credibility we have not
earned yet) and discovery — building, launching, living problems, until the
big thing reveals itself, often as a small thing that mutated. Discovery is
what we are doing now. We give every idea our best shot but marry the process,
not the idea: full commitment while we are on something, clean detachment when
it is time to let go. The kill-bar is the line that separates conviction from
stubbornness, and openness from never committing at all.

### Which bet we run

One bet at a time, picking up a second only when the first leaves us genuinely
waiting with capacity to spare. We do not agonise over which to pick — a wrong
pick dies in weeks. Bias to starting; the rest stay parked in the pool.

### How we run an experiment

1. **Channel sanity check first.** Before building anything: given the price
   this would command, is there any plausible channel that could ever reach
   people for it? If no channel could ever fit, the idea is dead before a day
   is spent.
2. **Scope is frozen once set** — in features and engineering both. Build the
   thing to work well for users, nothing more; not for scale up front.
   Tempting pieces that show up mid-build are not wrong, just early — parked.
   Changes that help users reach the frozen scope are fixes. Anything that
   grows what the product is, is a new idea, captured separately and run
   through this same process. Users name the pain, not the solution.
3. **Defining V1 is three steps, in order:** the hypothesis (the biggest thing
   this experiment is testing); the build (the minimum that must exist to
   prove or disprove it); build-versus-by-hand (backstage work defaults to
   manual; automate only when by-hand costs more than building).
4. **Fixed window, pre-registered bar.** Every experiment gets a fixed window
   decided up front, and before launch we write what would make it a yes: what
   a served user does, how often, how many real strangers. The line does not
   move afterward.
5. **First users by hand** — network, direct outreach, manual hustle. Bounded
   by time, not money. Paying for users here contaminates the signal. A polite
   friend is not a signal user.

### The verdict

Every experiment ends in one verdict against the pre-set bar: **continue**,
**kill**, or **blocked**. Blocked means it never truly ran — the only case
that earns more time. Before any kill, separate the two failures that look
alike: product failure (people tried it, it did not land) vs reach failure
(we could not get people to try it at all — the one that sank us last time).
We do not extend a window because results are thin. Thin results are a result.
If it is not a clear continue, it is a kill. Ambiguous is a kill. "Maybe there
is something here" is a kill. A clean kill with a real learning is the machine
working.

A continue does not mean chase scale. Growing is just more experiments through
this same engine — channels, pricing, features — each with its own hypothesis,
window, and bar. Scale is reinforced only when real usage demands it.

### Visibility and reflection

The work and its results stay visible to both of us: day-to-day work in small
scoped pieces whose status is current and shared, and every experiment's
verdict and the single thing it taught us in one shared record. Failing fast
is impossible if what is going wrong can hide.

## What is fixed, and what we improve

**The foundation is fixed**: the founder's final call and owning the outcome,
no relitigating once decided, discipline over motivation, fast
experimentation, the founder choosing the bets. Not up for revision.

**Everything else is an operating rule**, meant to improve as we learn — but
improving the document runs on the same discipline: a gap or better rule is
put in writing, the founder reads and decides, the change is logged. Never an
open debate.

## Operating rules (current)

1. **Hiring** — only against a clear test, all three parts holding: (a) the
   work is a clearly defined task we can hand off, not a vague need for help;
   (b) the time and effort to hire and train honestly beats the time it saves;
   (c) we can afford it without dropping runway below a year.
2. **Fundraising** — no chasing outside money until something is working. If
   we ever raise, it is to fuel something already proven, not to buy time to
   keep guessing.

## The Non-Negotiable Principles (enforceable list)

The numbered list every AI review and weekly violation report checks against:

1. **Validate before building.** No building beyond the smallest thing that
   tests the hypothesis.
2. **One bet at a time.** A second only when the first leaves us genuinely
   waiting.
3. **Scope frozen once set.** Growth requests are new ideas, not expansions.
4. **The bar is pre-registered and does not move.** Success defined before
   launch.
5. **Ambiguous is a kill.** Only a clear continue is a continue.
6. **Channel sanity check before any build.** Refuse to build the unsellable.
7. **First users by hand; no paid acquisition pre-signal.** Strangers count,
   friends do not.
8. **Big decisions get one written case, then a decision, then no
   relitigating.**
9. **Irreversible decisions always get the brake** (contracts, equity, legal,
   reputation, data loss, burned relationships → human review, always).
10. **Discipline over motivation.** The cadence runs whether or not it feels
    good this week.
11. **The work stays visible.** Statuses current, verdicts logged, learnings
    written.
12. **Hire only against the three-part test.**
13. **No outside money until something is working.**
